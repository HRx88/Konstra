ARCHITECTURE & DIGITAL FABRICATION

Learn how automation plays a role in the industry

Why Choose This Course?

This track trains you to design architecture that can actually be built by robots, and not just drawn beautifully on screen. You'll learn how geometry, materials, print paths, and construction constraints shape what is truly buildable, so your designs can be printed efficiently, safely, and at scale, especially in resource-limited and emerging-market environments.

About this Course

Architecture & Digital Fabrication is a 3-level pathway that bridges design intent with machine execution.

  • Level 1: builds your foundation in print constraints, materials behaviour, and reading simulations.
  • Level 2: teaches print-ready design rules, parametric workflows, and operator handoff documentation.
  • Level 3: focuses on scalable housing systems—optimising for cost, climate, speed, and community fit—so you can deliver production-ready solutions beyond one-off prototypes.

Career Pathways

Graduates may be suited for roles or pathways such as but not limited to:

  • Digital Fabrication Designer (assistant/junior)
  • Computational / Parametric Design Assistant (print-aware modelling)
  • Robotic Construction Design Coordinator (design-to-fabrication handoff support)
  • Architectural Technologist (digital fabrication workflows)
  • Fabrication Lab / Prototyping Specialist (construction-scale fabrication exposure)
  • Design-for-Manufacturing (DfMA) Assistant for Construction
  • Junior Project Designer for Affordable / Emerging-Market Housing (with further experience)

Entry Requirements

Age: This module is recommended to candidates aged 16 - 25, younger participants (13 - 15) are also encouraged to join if they can keep up with the pace and design exercises

Prior Experience: No prior experience in architecture, CAD, or 3D printing is required for Level 1. The track begins with visual rules and guided examples.

What You Will Learn

Level 1 - Foundations of Digital Fabrication Design

Target: Ages 13-30
Total Duration: 22 hours
Delivery Timeline: 4-6 weeks
Assessment: Concept & design rules test

Learning Outcomes

Upon completion of this course, learners will:
  • Identify unbuildable designs using basic print rules
  • Explain core geometry and material constraints for robotic construction
  • Critique architectural models for machine feasibility using simple simulations

The content curated is crafted in alignment with:
  • Polytechnic short courses
  • Intro certification standards
  • Parent expectations for commitment without overload

Learning Format:
  • Visual explainer videos
  • Diagrams, sketches, and simulations
  • Short design critique exercises
Module 1: Why Design for Machines, Not Just Looks
Outcomes:
  • Students can explain why visually attractive designs can fail when translated into robotic/print construction.
  • Students can identify common “machine-level” constraints that affect buildability (overhangs, thickness, stability).
  • Students can compare buildable vs unbuildable designs using simple before/after examples.
Lesson Time: 2.5 hours
Activities:
  • Explainer videos
  • Before/after design comparisons
  • Failure case visuals
Module 2: From Sketch to Machine Instructions
Outcomes:
  • Students can describe the workflow from concept → 3D model → slicing → toolpaths.
  • Students can explain what slicing and toolpaths represent in practical terms (how the machine “reads” geometry).
  • Students can recognise how scale changes feasibility (what works in small 3D prints may fail at building scale).
Lesson Time: 3 hours
Activities:
  • Workflow diagrams
  • Simple slicing demonstrations
  • Scale comparison examples
Module 3: Geometry Robots Can Actually Build
Outcomes:
  • Students can apply basic printability rules (minimum wall thickness, overhang limits, smooth geometry).
  • Students can identify geometry that increases risk of failure or excessive supports/extra steps.
  • Students can critique simple building forms for robotic feasibility using visual checks.
Lesson Time: 3.5 hours
Activities:
  • Printable vs non-printable sketches
  • Overhang and wall-thickness visual rules
  • Interactive shape comparisons
Module 4: Materials That Machines Can Use
Outcomes:
  • Students can explain what makes a construction material printable (flow + stability + bonding).
  • Students can identify common failure modes (sagging, cracking, poor bonding) and their likely causes.
  • Students can relate material behaviour to design decisions (why some shapes demand stronger material performance).
Lesson Time: 3 hours
Activities:
  • Material flow animations
  • Layer bonding visuals
  • Failure-mode examples
Module 5: Reading Print Simulations
Outcomes:
  • Students can interpret a sliced model and identify key parameters (layer height, path pattern, print time).
  • Students can predict how design edits can change print time, material use, and risk of defects.
  • Students can use simple simulation outputs to spot potential issues before printing.
Lesson Time: 4 hours
Activities:
  • Sliced-model walkthroughs
  • Print time estimation exercises
  • Parameter-change demonstrations
Module 6: Real Buildings That Were Actually Printed
Outcomes:
  • Students can summarise real printed building examples and what made them successful or risky.
  • Students can identify “design decisions that mattered” (geometry choices, print sequencing, simplifications).
  • Students can extract practical lessons that can be applied to future buildable design work.
Lesson Time: 3 hours
Activities:
  • Case study walkthroughs
  • Design decision breakdowns
  • Lessons-learned summaries
Module 7: Cost, Time, and Waste Basics
Outcomes:
  • Students can estimate basic cost drivers using simple rules (volume, print hours, labour, waste).
  • Students can compare designs based on efficiency (time/material) rather than aesthetics alone.
  • Students can explain why some elegant designs are expensive or inefficient to print at scale.
Lesson Time: 3 hours
Activities:
  • Cost logic diagrams
  • Simple volume & cost exercises
  • Design efficiency comparisons
Assessment
Assessment Time: 1 hours
Test Format:
Individual Project
  • Conceptual test on print rules
  • Critique of a sample 3D model for print feasibility

Level 2 - Design Rules for Robotic Construction

Target: Ages 13-30
Total Duration: 34 hours
Delivery Timeline: 6-8 weeks
Assessment: Print-ready redesign project

Learning Outcomes

Upon completion of this course, learners will:
  • Convert designs into print ready systems using design rules and parametric workflows
  • Simulate prints and optimise geometry for quality, speed, and material efficiency
  • Produce operator handoff packages with clear instructions and QC documentation

The content curated builds upon the skills of Level 1 with:
  • Increased technical depth
  • Stronger step-up from Level 1

Learning Format:
  • Guided tutorials
  • Applied design work
  • Simulations & case analysis
Module 1: Parametric Design for Printing
Outcomes:
  • Students can use parametric controls (sliders) to keep designs within printable ranges (thickness, radius, opening size).
  • Students can generate controlled design variations while maintaining buildability constraints.
  • Students can explain how parametric rules reduce printing failures and rework.
Lesson Time: 5 hours
Activities:
  • Rhino/Grasshopper demos
  • Slider-based parametric exercises
  • Constraint-driven modeling tasks
Module 2: Walls, Openings, and Connections
Outcomes:
  • Students can design openings (doors/windows) that remain structurally stable and printable.
  • Students can plan connections to foundations, slabs, and conventional elements with clear interface logic.
  • Students can identify weak junctions and propose adjustments to improve buildability and durability.
Lesson Time: 5 hours
Activities:
  • Structural opening examples
  • Joint-detail diagrams
  • Interface design walkthroughs
Module 3: Structural Logic in Printed Forms
Outcomes:
  • Students can explain basic load paths in printed structures and why reinforcement may be needed.
  • Students can identify where printed-only forms struggle (bending/tension) and propose hybrid solutions.
  • Students can apply reinforcement strategies at a concept level (ribs, fibers, embedded elements).
Lesson Time: 5.5 hours
Activities:
  • Load path diagrams
  • Reinforcement strategy visuals
  • Hybrid structure examples
Module 4: Layer-by-Layer Thinking
Outcomes:
  • Students can explain how path direction, sequencing, and layer stacking affect strength and print quality.
  • Students can identify causes of cold joints and propose design/sequence changes to reduce them.
  • Students can balance print speed vs quality using practical trade-off reasoning.
Lesson Time: 4.5 hours
Activities:
  • Path-direction simulations
  • Cold-joint case examples
  • Print-speed vs quality comparisons
Module 5: Material Efficiency and Waste
Outcomes:
  • Students can apply basic infill/section strategies to reduce material while maintaining performance.
  • Students can evaluate design options based on strength-to-material efficiency.
  • Students can identify waste sources (supports, over-thick sections, failed prints) and reduce them through design.
Lesson Time: 4 hours
Activities:
  • Infill strategy demos
  • Section studies
  • Density mapping exercises
Module 6: Environmental Design for Printing
Outcomes:
  • Students can describe how sun, wind, rain, and site access can affect printing outcomes.
  • Students can propose design and site adaptations that support print success in real conditions.
  • Students can integrate basic climate/site constraints into printable layout and sequencing decisions.
Lesson Time: 5 hours
Activities:
  • Climate-impact visuals
  • Wind/sun interaction simulations
  • Site access diagrams
Module 7: From Design to Shop Drawings
Outcomes:
  • Students can produce a clear print package (model, slices, parameters, QC checklist) for operators.
  • Students can communicate design intent with minimal ambiguity to robotic construction teams.
  • Students can identify missing information in handoffs and correct it using standard templates/checklists.
Lesson Time: 4 hours
Activities:
  • Print package templates
  • Operator handoff checklists
  • QC documentation examples
Assessment
Assessment Time: 1.5 hours (plus project work)
Test Format:
Group Project
  • Redesign an existing building to be print-ready
  • Deliverables: sliced model, design rationale, operator instructions

Level 3 - Architecture for Emerging Markets & Scale

Target: Ages 13-25 & Advanced students & professionals
Total Duration: 44 hours
Delivery Timeline: 8-10 weeks
Assessment: Full housing deployment design

Learning Outcomes

Upon completion of this course, learners will be:
  • Design scalable printed housing systems for emerging market contexts
  • Optimise designs for cost, climate performance, and fast deployment
  • Deliver production ready proposals at multi unit or neighbourhood scale

The content curated builds provides students with:
  • Industry-ready experience
  • Pre-immersion qualification

Learning Format:
  • Advanced instruction
  • Design & planning work
  • Critique & presentation
Module 1: Housing That Prints Fast and Cheap
Outcomes:
  • Students can design housing layouts optimised for speed, cost, and density (including shared walls).
  • Students can apply incremental housing logic (core now, expand later) to support real household needs.
  • Students can justify layout choices using material/time efficiency and usability constraints.
Lesson Time: 6 hours
Activities:
  • Dense housing case studies
  • Incremental housing models
  • Cost-driven layout exercises
Module 2: Climate-Responsive Printed Forms
Outcomes:
  • Students can propose design strategies for different climates (ventilation, shading, drainage, thermal performance).
  • Students can use parametric testing to evaluate design variations across climate conditions.
  • Students can explain how climate constraints shape geometry, sequencing, and detailing for printed builds.
Lesson Time: 6 hours
Activities:
  • Parametric climate testing
  • Ventilation and shading simulations
  • Roof and drainage studies
Module 3: Community and Cultural Fit
Outcomes:
  • Students can integrate cultural and household needs into printable housing layouts (privacy, cooking, family size).
  • Students can plan for future expansion without breaking printability or structural logic.
  • Students can apply a basic co-design approach to gather input and revise designs responsibly.
Lesson Time: 5.5 hours
Activities:
  • Cultural layout examples
  • Co-design workshop simulations
  • Expansion planning exercises
Module 4: Scaling From One House to Neighborhoods
Outcomes:
  • Students can plan multi-unit sequencing and optimise print schedules across several homes.
  • Students can identify opportunities for shared elements and infrastructure integration (utilities, walls, circulation).
  • Students can explain how neighbourhood-scale constraints change design and deployment strategy.
Lesson Time: 6.5 hours
Activities:
  • Multi-unit print sequencing
  • Shared wall optimization
  • Infrastructure integration diagrams
Module 5: Hybrid Construction Systems
Outcomes:
  • Students can design interfaces between printed components and conventional systems (roof, MEP, finishes).
  • Students can propose construction sequences that reduce risk and speed up delivery.
  • Students can detail critical connection points at a concept level to ensure buildability and coordination.
Lesson Time: 6 hours
Activities:
  • Printed + conventional interface detailing
  • Embedded systems walkthroughs
  • Construction sequencing logic
Module 6: Cost Modeling and Optimization
Outcomes:
  • Students can build a simple cost model linking design parameters to time, materials, and labour.
  • Students can run basic sensitivity checks to identify which design changes reduce cost/time the most.
  • Students can optimise designs iteratively while maintaining structural and usability requirements.
Lesson Time: 6 hours
Activities:
  • Spreadsheet-based cost models
  • Parameter-driven optimisation loops
  • Sensitivity analysis
Module 7: Capstone - Printed Housing Proposal
Lesson Time: 8 hours (guided + independent)
Activities:
Capstone project
  • Team-based design project
  • Client-style presentation
  • Full technical + community justification
