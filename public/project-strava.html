Why Cambodia?

Key Context

40% of Cambodia’s Urban population lives in slums

According to the Habitat for Humanity, approximately 40% of Cambodia’s urban population resides in slums based on data shared by UN-Habitat in 2023.

Although Cambodia achieved lower middle-income status in 2015, poverty rose again during the Covid-19 pandemic, pushing an estimated 460,000 Cambodians into poverty.

Approximately 4 in 10 people live in urban areas.

Habitat for Humanity

191 areas of poverty in Phnom Penh alone

According to Sahmakum Teang Tnaut (STT), a Phnom Penh based NGO, 191 locations urban locations have been identified as areas of poverty.

These communities are home to nearly 19,539 families which represents 89,879 people. This is 3.94% of Phnom Penh's population where many of them are in debt.

A fraction of those living in these areas also face pressure from eviction.

By 2030, one third of Cambodians will be living in cities

UN-Habitat's 2023 report projects that by 2030, one-third of Cambodia's entire population will be living in cities.

Urban growth is accelerating in Phnom Penh, Sihanoukville, Battambang, and Siem Reap, placing increased pressure on existing housing, drainage, infrastructure, and support systems.

This rapid shift highlights the urgency of ensuring that growing urban centers, especially Phnom Penh, are supported with sustainable and equitable development.

Where can Konstra help?

STUENG MEANCHEY

Our initial focus is the Stung Meanchey area within Khan Meanchey, Phnom Penh. This district is home to several vulnerable communities which includes Stung Meanchey I and II, which are located along the canal and contain clusters of modest housing built close to the waterway.

Although the canal underwent major clean-up works in 2023, nearby neighborhoods continue to face issues such as drainage stress during heavy rains and occasional land instability, as seen in recent road collapses caused by erosion.

Historically, the area surrounding the former Stung Meanchey landfill was home to families who relied on waste-scavenging to survive, underscoring the long-standing economic vulnerability of parts of this district. While Stung Meanchey III, which is generally more built-up based on current urban development patterns, still contains pockets of households that remain economically fragile and would benefit from additional support.

Development Area

Our project aims to develop two specific locations within Stung Meanchey II. Point A (outlined in orange) covers approximately 5,673 m², while Point B (outlined in yellow) spans an estimated 6,000 m² of land.

The development area lies just south of the former Stung Meanchey dumpsite and near Lenkov Village, a relocation community established to support families who previously lived around the landfill.

These locations were selected because of their proximity to existing residential clusters, including households relocated from the former dumpsite. Developing in this area helps maintain community continuity, supports families that remain economically vulnerable, and aligns with the district’s ongoing urban redevelopment. The chosen land pockets are currently underutilized, making them well-suited for smaller-scale, community-focused housing projects that can integrate smoothly with surrounding infrastructure and social networks.

Partnerships and Collaborations

Stung Meanchey has long been supported by several NGOs working to improve community well-being, including the Cambodian Children’s Fund (CCF), People Improvement Organization (PIO), and ISF Cambodia.

These organisations provide essential services such as education, family support, and community assistance that help strengthen local livelihoods.

However, despite the presence of long-standing NGOs that have improved conditions for many families, physical infrastructure and affordable housing remain notable gaps. Several pockets within Stung Meanchey II still contain sub-optimal structures, limited drainage resilience, and economically vulnerable households recovering from the transition based livelihoods.

Our Solution

To address development gaps within the district, Konstra aims to introduce a sustainable 3D-printed housing solution in Stueng Meanchey II. By partnering with established local NGOs, we seek to complement existing community efforts and provide safe, durable homes that support long-term well-being. Our goal is to help cultivate an environment where families can live securely, grow together, and truly call the community their home.

3D Printing Concrete?

3D concrete printing is an additive manufacturing technique used in construction. It produces a three-dimensional structure by extruding concrete layer by layer based on a digital model.

This allows walls and structural elements to be formed without traditional formwork, which normally requires building timber molds to shape and hold concrete until it cures.

Compared to conventional construction, 3D concrete printing greatly reduces waste by depositing only the exact amount of material needed for each section. The automated layer-by-layer process shortens construction time, eliminates labour-intensive steps such as assembling and removing formwork, and reduces dependence on large on-site teams.

By removing the need for timber formwork, it also lowers material costs, minimizes resource use, and reduces deforestation linked to formwork production. Additionally, 3D concrete printing can operate in harsher or hazardous environments, improving worker safety while maintaining consistent build quality.

PrintAdobe

PrintAdobe is Konstra’s skill-building initiative aimed at empowering the Stueng Meanchey community. Through structured workshops, residents and local students are trained in 3D-printed construction learning how to prepare materials, operate printing systems, monitor print quality, and maintain equipment.

By integrating local participation into the construction process, PrintAdobe creates meaningful employment, equips youth with industry-relevant technical skills, and ensures community ownership of the housing development. Over time, this initiative strengthens long-term sustainability by enabling residents to support future maintenance, expansion, and new builds independently.
